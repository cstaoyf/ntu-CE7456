Today, we will discuss the {\em subgraph enumeration} problem (also known a {\em subgraph listing}). Let $G = (V, E)$ be an undirected graph called the {\em data graph}. A graph $G' = (V', E')$ is a {\em subgraph} of $G$ if $V' \subseteq V$ and $E' \subseteq E$. Let $Q = (V_Q, E_Q)$ be another undirected graph called the {\em pattern graph}. A subgraph $G' = (V', E')$ of $G$ is {\em isomorphic} to $Q$ if there is a bijection function $f: V_Q \rightarrow V'$ such that, for any distinct vertices $u, v \in V'$, the pattern graph $Q$ has an edge $\set{u, v}$ if and only if the edge $\set{f(u), f(v)}$ exists in $G'$. The goal of subgraph enumeration is to report all the subgraphs of $G$ that are isomorphic to $Q$. These subgraphs are called the {\em occurrences} of $Q$.

\vgap

We assume that $Q$ has a constant size, i.e., the number $|V_Q|$ of its vertices can be regarded as a constant (and, hence, so can the number $|E_Q|$ of its edges). This is necessary; otherwise, the problem is known to be NP-hard: finding whether $G$ has a clique is NP-hard.

\extraspacing {\bf Math Conventions.} For an integer $x \ge 1$, the notation $[x]$ represents the set $\set{1, 2, ..., x}$. The notation $\real_{\ge 0}$ represents the set of non-negative real numbers.

\section{Fractional Edge Cover Number} \label{sec:frac-edge-cover-num}

A function $w: E_Q \rightarrow \real_{\ge 0}$ is said to be a {\em fractional edge cover} of $Q = (V_Q, E_Q)$ if, for every vertex $u \in V_Q$, it holds that
\myeqn{
    \sum_{e \in E_Q: u \in e} w(e) &\ge& 1. \label{eqn:frac-edge-cover}
}
We refer to $w(e)$ as the {\em weight} of $e$ under $w$.
The {\em total weight} of $w$ is defined as
\myeqn{
    \sum_{e \in E_Q} w(e). \nn
}
The {\em fractional edge cover number} of $Q$ --- denoted as $\rho(Q)$ --- is defined as the smallest total weight of all fractional edge covers of $Q$. A fractional edge cover is said to be {\em optimal} if its total weight is $\rho(Q)$.

\vgap

For example, if $Q$ is a $k$-cycle (i.e., a cycle with $k$ edges), then $\rho(Q) = k/2$; if $Q$ is a $k$-star (i.e., a graph with $k + 1$ vertices $u, v_1, ..., v_k$, and $k$ edges $\set{u, v_1}, \set{u, v_2}, ..., \set{u, v_k}$), then $\rho(Q) = k$.

\vgap

The following is known as the {\em AGM bound}:

\begin{theorem} \label{thm:agm}
    If the data graph $G$ has $m$ edges and the pattern graph $Q$ has a constant size, then $G$ can have $O(m^{\rho(Q)})$ occurrences of $Q$.
\end{theorem}

The AGM bound is tight:

\begin{theorem} \label{thm:agm-lb}
    Fix any integer $m \ge 1$ and any pattern graph $Q$ with a constant size. There is a data graph $G$ with at most $m$ edges that has $\Omega(m^{\rho(Q)})$ occurrences of $Q$.
\end{theorem}

We will prove Theorem~\ref{thm:agm} in this lecture and Theorem~\ref{thm:agm-lb} in a future lecture of this course.

\section{Enumerating Odd-Length Cycles} \label{sec:cycle}

Let $Q$ be a $k$-cycle with an odd number $k = O(1)$. In this section, we will give an algorithm to find all its occurrences in a data graph $G$ of $m$ edges in $O(m^{\rho(Q)})$ time where $\rho(Q) = k/2$.

\vgap

Call a vertex $u$ of $G$ {\em heavy}, if the degree of $u$ is at least $\sqrt{m}$, or {\em light}, otherwise. The number of heavy vertices of $G$ is at most $2 \sqrt{m}$ (otherwise, $G$ would have more than $m$ edges, giving a contradiction).

\vgap

Let us denote the vertices in $Q$ as $q_1, q_2, ..., q_k$, respectively. To find an occurrence of $Q$, we need to map each $q_i$ ($i \in [k]$) to a distinct vertex in $G$. We consider two types of mappings:

\extraspacing {\bf Type 1: All Vertices of $Q$ Mapped to Heavy Vertices.} There are only $O((\sqrt{m})^k) = O(m^{k/2})$ such mappings because the number of heavy vertices is bounded by $O(\sqrt{m})$. For each mapping, we can check in constant time whether the subgraph of $G$ induced by the mapping is indeed an occurrence of $Q$. The time of finding all such occurrences is therefore $O(m^{k/2})$.

\extraspacing {\bf Type 2: At least One Vertex of $Q$ Is Mapped to a Light Vertex.} W.l.o.g., assume that $q_1$ must be mapped to a light vertex. We consider all such mappings $f: V_Q \rightarrow V$ satisfying three conditions:
\myenums{
    \item $f(q_1)$ is a light vertex;
    \item $\set{f(q_1), f(q_2)}$ and $\set{f(q_1), f(q_k)}$ are edges in $G$;
    \item for each $i \in \set{2, 3, ..., (k-1)/2}$, $\set{f(q_{2i-1}), f(q_{2i})}$ is an edge in $G$.
}
There are $O(m^{1.5})$ choices to satisfy conditions 1 and 2 (think: why?), while there are $O(m^{\fr{k-1}{2}-1})$ choices to satisfy condition 3. Hence, the total number of such mappings is bounded by $O(m^{k/2})$. For each mapping, we can check in constant time whether it induces an occurrence of $Q$. The time of finding all such occurrences is therefore $O(m^{k/2})$.

\section{Enumerating Stars} \label{sec:star}

Let $Q$ be a $k$-star where $k$ is a constant integer at least 1. It is rudimentary to find all its occurrences in a data graph $G$ of $m$ edges in $O(m^{\rho(Q)})$ time where $\rho(Q) = k$. The details are left to you as an exercise.

\section{Enumerating Arbitrary Subgraphs} \label{sec:enum-gen}

Let $Q = (V_Q, E_Q)$ be a pattern graph with a constant size. Given a fractional edge cover $w$ of $Q$, we define the {\em support of $w$} as
\myeqn{
    \supp(w) &=&
    \{
        e \in E_Q \mid w(e) > 0
    \}. \label{eqn:supp-w}
}
Note that, for each vertex $u \in V_Q$, at least one edge in $\supp(w)$ is incident on $u$.

\begin{theorem} \label{thm:decomp}
   Every pattern graph $Q$ admits an optimal fractional edge cover $w^*$ whose $\supp(w^*)$ induces a collection of vertex-disjoint odd-length cycles $\mycycle_1, ..., \mycycle_\alpha$ ($\alpha \ge 0$) and stars $\mystar_1, ..., \mystar_\beta$ ($\beta \ge 0$) such that $\rho(Q) = \sum_{i=1}^\alpha \rho(\mycycle_i) + \sum_{i=1}^\beta \rho(\mystar_i)$.
\end{theorem}

We will prove the theorem in the next section. For an illustration, Figure~\ref{fig:fish}(a) shows a pattern graph $Q$ with $\rho(Q) = 5.5$, while Figure~\ref{fig:fish}(b) shows the promised collection, which has $\alpha = 1$ odd-length cycle and $\beta = 2$ stars. It is easy to verify that the sum of the fractional edge cover numbers of the cycle and stars is precisely 5.5.

\vgap

Theorem~\ref{thm:decomp} implies the following algorithm to find the occurrences of $Q$ in a data graph $G = (V, E)$:
\myitems{
    \item For each $i \in [\alpha]$, find the set $M_i^{\mycycle}$ of occurrences of $\mycycle_i$. We consider $M_i^{\mycycle}$ to be a set of functions, each mapping the vertex set of $\mycycle_i$ to $V$.

    \item For each $i \in [\beta]$, find the set $M_i^{\mystar}$ of occurrences of $\mystar_i$. We consider $M_i^{\mystar}$ to be a set of functions, each mapping the vertex set of $\mystar_i$ to $V$.

    \item For each $(f_1^{\mycycle}, ..., f_\alpha^{\mycycle}, f_1^{\mystar}, ..., f_\beta^{\mystar}) \in (M_1^{\mycycle}, ..., M_\alpha^{\mycycle}, M_1^{\mystar}, ..., M_\beta^{\mystar})$, check in constant time whether $(f_1^{\mycycle}, ..., f_\alpha^{\mycycle}, f_1^{\mystar}, ..., f_\beta^{\mystar})$ induces an occurrence of $Q$.
}
By the discussion in Sections~\ref{sec:cycle} and \ref{sec:star}, the running time is at the order of
\myeqn{
    \prod_{i=1}^\alpha m^{\rho(\mycycle_i)}
    \cdot
    \prod_{i=1}^\beta m^{\rho(\mystar_i)}
    \nn
}
which is $O(m^{\rho(Q)})$ by Theorem~\ref{thm:decomp}.

\vgap

As a side product, we have given a constructive proof of the AGM bound in Theorem~\ref{thm:agm}.

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:decomp}} \label{sec:proof-thm-decomp}

We will prove the theorem through a series of lemmas that reveal non-trivial properties of fractional edge covers.

\begin{lemma} \label{lmm:proof-h1}
   Suppose that $Q = (V_Q, E_Q)$ has an optimal fractional edge cover $w^*$ whose $\supp(w^*)$ induces a star. Then, $w^*$ maps each edge of the star to 1 and every other edge of $E_Q$ to 0.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
   Trivial and omitted.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma} \label{lmm:proof-h2}
   Suppose that $Q = (V_Q, E_Q)$ has an optimal fractional edge cover $w^*$ whose $\supp(w^*)$ induces a forest. Then, $Q$ has an optimal fractional edge cover $w$ such that
   \myitems{
        \item $\supp(w)$ induces a collection of stars.
        \item $w(e) = 1$ for each $e \in \supp(w)$.
   }
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Let $\T$ be the forest induced by $\supp(w^*)$. If $\T$ is a star, the claim follows immediately from Lemma~\ref{lmm:proof-h1}. Next, we assume that $\T$ is not a star.

    \vgap

    We prove the lemma by induction on $|\supp(w^*)|$. The base case where $|\supp(w^*)| = 1$ holds because in that case $\T$ is a star. Next, assuming that the lemma holds when $|\supp(w^*)| = t$, we will prove the claim for $|\supp(w^*)| = t+1$.

    \vgap

    Root $\T$ at an arbitrary degree-1 node. Let $z$ be a deepest leaf of $\T$ (i.e., its distance from the root is the largest among all leaves). Take an arbitrary leaf $z$ of $\T$, and let $u_1$ be its parent. The value of $w^*(\set{z, u_1})$ must be 1.

    \vgap

    Let $u_2$ be the parent of $u_1$, and $u_3$ be the parent of $u_2$ (think: why can't $u_1$ and $u_2$ be the root?). We modify $w^*$ as follows:
    \myitems{
        \item increase $w^*(\set{u_2, u_3})$ by $w^*(\set{u_1, u_2})$;
        \item set $w^*(\set{u_1, u_2})$ to 0.
    }
    The new $w^*$ is an optimal fractional edge cover of $Q$ with $|\supp(w^*)| = t$ (because $\set{u_1, u_2}$ is no longer in $\supp(w^*)$). The claim now follows from our inductive assumption.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma} \label{lmm:proof-h3}
   If $Q = (V_Q, E_Q)$ is a bipartite graph. It has an optimal fractional edge cover $w^*$ such that $w^*(e)$ is either 0 or 1 for each $e \in E_Q$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    By Lemma~\ref{lmm:proof-h2}, it suffices to prove that $Q$ has an optimal fractional edge cover $w^*$ whose $\supp(w^*)$ induces a forest. If this is not true, then the subgraph of $Q$ induced by $\supp(w^*)$ must contain a cycle $\mycycle$. As $Q$ is bipartite, $\mycycle$ must have an even number of edges, which we denote by $e_1, e_2, ..., e_k$ in some traversal order. W.l.o.g., suppose that $e_1$ has the smallest weight under $w^*$ among $e_1, e_2, ..., e_k$. Set $\Delta = w^*(e_1)$. We now modify $w^*$ by
    \myitems{
        \item decreasing the weights of $e_1, e_3, ..., e_{k-1}$ by $\Delta$;
        \item increasing the weights of $e_2, e_4, ..., e_{k}$ by $\Delta$.
    }
    The new $w^*$ is an optimal fractional edge cover of $Q$ whose $\supp(w^*)$ has one less edge (because $e_1$ is no longer in $\supp(w^*)$).

    \vgap

    If the subgraph induced by $\supp(w^*)$ still has a cycle, we repeat the above process by further reducing $\supp(w^*)$. This eventually gives a an optimal fractional edge cover $w^*$ of $Q$ whose $\supp(w^*)$ induces a forest.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma} \label{lmm:proof-h4}
   Any pattern graph $Q = (V_Q, E_Q)$ has an optimal fractional edge cover $w^*$ such that $w^*(e) \in \set{0, 1/2, 1}$ for each $e \in E_Q$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
   Let us build a bipartite graph $Q' = (V_{Q'}, E_{Q'})$ as follows. For each vertex $u \in V_Q$, add two vertices $u_L$ and $u_R$ to $V_{Q'}$ --- refer to them as the {\em left copy} and {\em right copy} of $u$, respectively. For each edge $\set{u, v} \in E_Q$, add two edges $\set{u_L, v_R}$ and $\set{v_L, u_R}$ to $E_{Q'}$, where the subscripts indicate whether a vertex is a left or right copy.

   \vgap

   By Lemma~\ref{lmm:proof-h3}, $Q'$ has an optimal fractional edge cover $w^*$ such that $w^*(e) \in \set{0, 1}$ for every $e \in E_{Q'}$. Define a function $w: E_Q \rightarrow \real_{\ge 0}$ by setting, for each $e \in E_Q$, $w(e) = \fr{1}{2}(w^*(e_L) + w^*(e_R))$. We claim that $w$ is a fractional edge cover of $Q$. Indeed, for any vertex $u \in V_Q$, we have
   \myeqn{
        && \sum_{v \in V_Q: \set{u, v} \in E_{Q}} w(\set{u, v}) \nn \\
        &=&
        \fr{1}{2}
        \sum_{v \in V_Q: \set{u, v} \in E_{Q}}
        w^*(\set{u_L, v_R}) +
        w^*(\set{v_L, u_R}) \nn \\
        &=&
        \fr{1}{2}
        \sum_{v_R \in V_{Q'}: \set{u_L, v_R} \in E_{Q'}}
        w^*(\set{u_L, v_R})
        +
        \fr{1}{2}
        \sum_{v_L \in V_{Q'}: \set{v_L, u_R} \in E_{Q'}}
        w^*(\set{v_L, u_R})
        \nn \\
        &\ge&
        1 \nn
   }
   where the last step used the fact that $w^*$ is a fractional edge cover of $Q'$.
\end{proof}
